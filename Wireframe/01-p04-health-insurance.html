Detail článku (L4)

Health insurance is mandatory for everyone living in the Czech Republic. There are two systems — public (state-run) and commercial. Which one you need depends on your residence status, employment situation, and family ties.

▶

How does health insurance work for foreigners in CZ?

Czech health insurance covers most medical needs — from GP visits to hospital stays, prescriptions, and emergency care. There are two parallel systems:

  • Public health insurance (veřejné zdravotní pojištění) — managed by 7 state-recognized insurance funds. Covers everyone with employment, self-employment, EU family ties, or certain protected statuses.
  • Commercial health insurance (komerční zdravotní pojištění) — private insurance products, mandatory for long-term visa holders and dependents who don't qualify for the public system.

What it covers

  • GP visits and basic care
  • Specialist consultations
  • Hospital admissions and surgery
  • Prescription co-pays
  • Dental basics (extractions, basic restorations)
  • Pregnancy & birth
  • Child healthcare

Who is eligible?

Your residence status determines which system you fall under:

How to get covered

  1. Choose a provider (see list below)
  2. Submit registration (employer handles for employees)
  3. Get your insurance card
  4. Register with a GP near you

How to get covered

  1. 1

    Choose a public insurance provider

    Pay attention to specific conditions for foreigners — some insurers have stronger English support, faster registration for new arrivals, or specialised packages for expat families.

    Pick from the list of providers
  2. 2

    Submit your registration

    If you're employed, your employer handles this automatically when reporting your work start to your chosen insurer. Self-employed and unemployed need to register in person at the insurer's branch within 8 days of starting work or losing previous coverage.

    Insurance registration form (PDF, 220 KB)
  3. 3

    Get your insurance card

    Your insurer issues a green plastic card (or European Health Insurance Card for EU/EEA citizens). It usually arrives by post within 2–3 weeks of registration. Carry it to every medical appointment — doctors will not bill you directly if your card is valid.

  4. 4

    Register with a GP near you

    Once you have insurance, register with a general practitioner (and a paediatrician for children) in your area. They become your first contact for all non-emergency care and the gateway to specialists.

Required documents

Required documents ▾

To register for public insurance, you'll typically need:

  • Passport or national ID
  • Residence permit (visa, biometric card)
  • Employment contract or trade licence
  • Birth certificate (for children)
  • Marriage certificate (for family-tied coverage)
  • S1 / E106 form (EU students)

Frequently asked questions

I lost my job — what happens to my insurance? ▾

You have a grace period to register with a new employer or as a job seeker (Úřad práce). Without this, you must pay public insurance yourself or risk being uninsured.

Can I keep my home country insurance? ▾

Generally no — Czech residents must have Czech-recognized insurance. EU citizens can use EHIC for temporary coverage, but long-term stay requires switching.

My partner has Czech insurance, am I covered? ▾

Only if you're an EU family member, dependent child, or in specific protected categories. Otherwise, you need your own insurance.

I'm pregnant — does my insurance cover birth? ▾

Public insurance: fully. Commercial: depends on package — many treat pregnancy as add-on or exclude it. Check before pregnancy if possible.

I came on a tourist visa and got injured — what now? ▾

Emergency care is provided regardless. You'll receive a bill. Your travel insurance should cover it; check with your provider.

Can I switch insurance providers? When? ▾

Yes — public insurance allows switches twice a year (1 January or 1 July), with 3 months notice.

Why is my dental work not fully covered? ▾

Public insurance covers basic dental (extractions, basic restorations) but not aesthetic or advanced procedures. Many people add commercial dental insurance.
